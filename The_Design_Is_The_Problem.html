Morning · push notifications

She checks her phone before her feet touch the floor.

Three notifications from last night — one sent at 2:30 AM. She opens Instagram. The first thing she sees is a photo of her classmates at a gathering she wasn't invited to.

The mechanism

Push notifications triggered the return visit. The algorithm surfaced socially activating content first — not because that's what she wanted to see, but because it generates the strongest engagement response. The feed chose for her.

Morning · infinite scroll

Forty minutes have passed. She didn't decide to spend that time.

A fitness influencer. A mental health meme. A promoted post about anxiety medication. The scroll has no stopping point. There is no bottom of the page.

The mechanism

Unlike a book or a newspaper, the feed is engineered to eliminate the moment where a person would naturally stop and put it down. Infinite scroll removes all stopping cues by design.

Midday · algorithmic learning

The algorithm learned she pauses on content about loneliness.

At lunch, TikTok opens to a video that autoplays immediately. Twenty minutes pass. She puts her phone away feeling worse than before she picked it up.

The mechanism

Autoplay removes the deliberate choice to consume more. The algorithm's learning creates a feedback loop: pausing on loneliness content trains the system to show more of it. The platform interprets distress as engagement.

Night · late-night re-engagement

A notification reminds her of a comment she hasn't responded to.

She can't sleep. She opens her phone. The blue light and the feed begin again. She feels anxious if she doesn't reply. The cycle continues.

The mechanism

Late-night engagement disrupts sleep — a critical factor in adolescent mental health. Social obligation mechanics create anxiety about disengaging. SB-976 now prohibits notifications to minors between midnight and 6 AM for precisely this reason.

04 — The system

It is the environment,
not the individual.

Social work's ecological perspective insists on this: individual outcomes often have structural causes. A teenager's anxiety is not simply her own — it is produced, at population scale, by a design environment engineered for something other than her wellbeing.

Advertising-driven revenue rewards engagement.

Macro

At the outermost ring: the market. Platforms generate revenue proportional to time-on-platform. Until recently, no regulations set design-safety standards for digital products used by minors. The business model is the root cause.

ad revenue no design standards engagement incentives

Features are optimized to hold attention, not to serve users.

Mezzo

The middle ring: architecture. Algorithmic feed curation, notification systems, autoplay mechanics, engagement metrics, and content recommendation engines shape how young people experience digital environments. These are not individual choices — they are design decisions.

algorithmic feeds notifications autoplay engagement loops

Outcomes appear individual. They are produced structurally.

Micro

The innermost ring: the teenager herself. Sleep disruption. Social comparison. Difficulty disengaging. Anxiety. These outcomes appear individual — but they are produced by the design environment at a population scale. This is why "just use your phone less" does not work.

05 — Leverage points

Change the environment,
not the child.

We do not ask children to individually resist lead paint, unsafe playground equipment, or tobacco advertising. We change the environment. Three laws — two already on the books in California, one advancing in Congress — mark what it looks like to treat platform design as a public health issue.

Target the addiction, not the speech.

Effective Feb. 2025 · upheld by the Ninth Circuit Sept. 2025

SB-976 directly targets the design features that create addictive digital environments for minors. Rather than moderating content, it restructures the conditions under which young people encounter social media.

  • Prohibits algorithmically curated "addictive feeds" for minors without verifiable parental consent.

  • Blocks notifications to minors between midnight and 6 AM, and during school hours.

  • Requires default private account settings for every minor user.

  • Hides engagement metrics — likes, shares — from minors by default.

A warning label, like the cigarette pack.

Signed Oct. 2025 · effective January 2027

Modeled on tobacco and alcohol health warnings, AB-56 mandates that platforms display health-risk warnings to users under 18 — adapting a proven public health strategy to the digital environment.

  • Skippable 10-second warning when a minor first logs on each day.

  • Unskippable 30-second warning after 3 hours of cumulative use.

  • Warning repeats after each additional hour of use.

  • Language drawn from the Surgeon General's 2023 Advisory.

The first federal duty of care since COPPA.

Reintroduced May 2025 · KIDS Act advanced March 2026

KOSA establishes a federal "duty of care" requiring platforms to prevent and mitigate harms to minors — the first proposed federal standard for platform design safety since COPPA in 1998.

  • Creates a duty of care requiring platforms to act in the best interest of minors.

  • Disables algorithmic feeds and addictive features by default for minors.

  • Restricts geolocation features for users under 18.

  • Passed the U.S. Senate 91–3 with overwhelming bipartisan support.

06 — Why this matters

This is not about restricting speech. It is not about blaming young people. It is about establishing minimum safety standards for the environments in which millions of developing minds spend their lives.

i

The problem is structural.

Harmful design features — not just harmful content — produce population-level effects. Intervention must address the environment.

ii

Policy is a legitimate tool.

SB-976, AB-56, and KOSA represent evidence-informed approaches grounded in established public health principles.

iii

Social work is needed here.

Systems thinking, ecological analysis, and prevention orientation are precisely what this issue requires.

iv

Change is underway.

The Ninth Circuit upheld SB-976. California signed AB-56. KOSA passed the Senate 91–3. The policy window is open.
